2. Historical Context of Ethnic Representation in Kenya

From the moment Kenya gained independence in 1963, ethnic representation in government has been both a political bargaining chip and a flashpoint for national unity.

2.1 Early Independence Era (1963–1978)

Under President Jomo Kenyatta, the federal (“Majimbo”) structure was quickly dismantled. Power was centralised in the executive, and senior government positions increasingly reflected the President’s ethnic base.

2.2 Moi Era and “Ethnic Balancing” (1978–2002)

President Daniel arap Moi introduced informal ethnic balancing in Cabinet and top positions, rewarding loyalty rather than enforcing fairness. This entrenched elite-driven patronage networks, despite providing visibility for smaller groups.

2.3 Transition to Multiparty Politics (1991–2002)

Political parties formed along ethnic lines, relying on “kingpins” to deliver bloc votes in return for power-sharing and representation in state institutions.

2.4 Kibaki Era and Post-2007 Crisis (2002–2013)

After early merit-based reforms, President Kibaki’s administration regressed into ethnic favouritism. The disputed 2007 election led to violence and power-sharing through the National Accord and Grand Coalition Government, where appointments were split ethnically.

2.5 2010 Constitution – A New Legal Framework

The Constitution introduced values of inclusion and fairness (Articles 10, 27, 56, 232), but lacked statutory enforcement mechanisms to ensure proportional representation or penalise breaches.

2.6 Recent Developments (2013–2024)

Despite constitutional provisions, certain ethnic groups remain overrepresented in Cabinet, parastatals, and top offices. Coalition bargains persist, dividing state power based on bloc politics, undermining equality and inclusion.
