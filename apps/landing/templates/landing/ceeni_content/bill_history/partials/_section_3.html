3. Patterns of Tribalism in Politics and Governance

Ethnic identity has remained a central organising principle in Kenya’s political and administrative systems. While ethnicity is a legitimate marker of cultural identity, its politicisation has created patterns that distort governance and weaken national cohesion.

3.1 Ethnic Political Parties and “Kingpins”

Political parties often form around dominant ethnic leaders who negotiate power-sharing deals. These “kingpins” command bloc votes in exchange for Cabinet posts and public sector control.

3.2 Coalition Arithmetic

Winning elections relies on combining large ethnic blocs. These arrangements continue post-election, determining which communities get which ministries or parastatal appointments.

3.3 Cabinet and Senior Appointments as Political Rewards

High-level appointments serve as political currency. While appearing inclusive, these often benefit elite insiders rather than the wider community.

3.4 Marginalisation of “Non-Strategic” Communities

Communities seen as politically “weak” or scattered are frequently excluded from high-level posts, despite constitutional protections. They lack bargaining power in ethnic coalitions.

3.5 The Culture of Patronage and “Our Turn to Eat”

Public office is viewed as a reward. The informal mantra “ni wakati wetu wa kula” drives zero-sum politics, undermining national belonging and meritocracy.

3.6 Impact on Public Trust and National Cohesion

Ethnic bias in appointments erodes trust, fuels grievances, and destabilises elections. Many communities feel alienated from government, weakening Kenya’s cohesion.
